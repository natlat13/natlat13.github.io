Hurricane Safety and Preparedness

These Families Chose to Stay Home During a Hurricane. Here's How They Fared.

By Ally Hirschlag

June 05 2020 12:30 PM EDT

weather.com

Choosing to evacuate before a potentially devastating hurricane makes landfall is a prudent decision, especially if evacuation orders have been issued for nearby areas. However, many people decide to weather the storm at home. In New Orleans, during Hurricane Katrina, for example, it's estimated that up to 30% of the population stayed put because they simply weren't able to leave. Some were physically incapable, like the elderly and people with disabilities, others were economically unable â meaning they couldn't afford lodging or travel out of the city.

Over half of those who chose to stay, though, did so because they underestimated the severity of the storm, and assumed they'd have no trouble riding it out. This is a commonly cited reason why people choose to stay home when a hurricane is imminent â especially in regions that get hit by hurricanes on a regular basis, like Southern Florida, or cities situated on the Gulf of Mexico. They assume that if they've made it through several hurricanes relatively unscathed, why should they change their shelter plans now?

One reason to consider is that scientists are seeing hurricanes getting stronger as climate change escalates.

That said, sticking out a hurricane can be perfectly safe, especially if your home is built to withstand high winds, flooding and flying debris. Here's how three families who chose to stay home made it through hurricanes relatively unscathed.

In South Florida

Dominique Martinez, a Director of SEO in Fort Lauderdale, Florida has seen her fair share of dangerous, unpredictable weather, including hurricanes. "Typically, we decide to stick it out because we experience these things all the time," says Martinez. "If you chose to leave every time there was a hurricane warning, youâd be traveling to and from your home a lot during hurricane season."

However, she admits that things can get quite dicey if you choose to stay when a particularly intense hurricane is on the way. "During one hurricane we sustained, we didnât have power for 2 weeks," she recalls. "Schools were cancelled and most places were closed, all but the main grocery stores and gas stations." During the worst of it, Martinez says you can hear the wind howling and cracking the trees, and the rain getting heavier and smacking the windows. Tree branches and debris whip back and forth in the wind.

After the storm, access to clean water can become limited unless you have an expensive filtration system or if you've got enough bottled water to last at least a week.

But that wasn't the worst of it.

"The scariest part of it all isnât the storm, but all of the people panicking as they prepare for the storm. Grocery stores are always wiped of everything non-perishable, especially water," she continues. "Thatâs always the first thing to fly off the shelves."

In North Florida

Jennifer Walden, Director of Operations at WikiLawn Lawn Care in Texas, had first-hand experience with Hurricane Irma. They decided to stay for a number of reasons, including financial stress and lack of available places to shelter that would welcome their pets. "We're definitely not the type to just wait it out and downplay a hurricane," says Walden.

Their home at the time was not particularly sturdy, which concerned Walden. "We lived in a mobile home that had essentially been strapped down to a foundation, I was genuinely afraid the house wouldn't survive it."

Amazingly, their mobile home barely moved. They did sustain some tree damage, but it was nothing serious. This was likely due to the preparations they made ahead of the hurricane. "We boarded up the windows the day before the storm came, since it took that long to be certain it was going to take the path it did (which was another reason for our hesitation in leaving)," she explains. "We of course also brought everything inside, and my husband took down a branch near the house that was mostly dead and just barely hanging on."

While they made it through the storm relatively unscathed, Walden remembers having moments of wishing they'd found a way to leave. "The worst of the winds hit us between 1 and 2am, if I remember right, which is a terrifying time to experience that. I definitely wished we'd left then."

Since Irma, the Waldens moved to Texas, which does see far fewer hurricanes, and reside in a home with a solid foundation.

In Central Florida

As a fellow Floridian, Sophia Antoine, a life coach from Arcadia is no stranger to hurricanes. "Each time a hurricane comes, we prefer to be at home because weâve already been through the worst," she says. While they were living in Fort Lauderdale, their minivan was totaled during Hurricane Wilma, and the family had no power or potable water for two weeks, but Antoine says, after they survived hurricane Charley the previous year, Wilma's impact didn't seem so bad.

"Charley wasnât supposed to cause so much damage to Arcadia," says Antoine. "It made a last minute turn and we were just stuck."

Antoine has one particularly vivid memory of Charley â stepping outside when she thought the storm had passed only to quickly learn she was seriously mistaken.

"I can remember opening my back door and suddenly realizing that it wasnât over, we were in just in the eye. It was like nothing ever happened. There were a few displaced trees and limbs, but aside from that, things looked normal. I realized it wasn't over when the wind picked up and it was more fierce than before. I could see the trees moving similar to a cartoon when the Tasmanian Devil is far off and is approaching quickly. When I tried to close the door, I couldnât. It took me and my pregnant cousin to push it closed."

After it was really over, Antoine remembers walking outside into knee-high water, and seeing tree branches and other debris floating down the street. "I recall every other roof being torn off and pieces of wood and siding in the street," she says. "I have friends who lost their houses and lived in the FEMA park [mobile home park FEMA built for hurricane victims] until they could get insurance payouts."

Despite her experience, Antoine wouldn't change how she preps for storms, including choosing to stay home in most cases. She makes sure there is enough food and water for her family and pets. They also have a generator now since her husband had a kidney transplant in 2015, and requires access to electric-powered medical equipment. But even though she knows how to prepare for hurricanes, Antoine says there's no way to completely protect yourself from such a volatile, major storm if you stay in its path.

"I've been through enough storms to know that they are unpredictable and we pray for the best each time."
